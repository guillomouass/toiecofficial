Grammar · Lesson 1

Present Perfect vs. Past Simple

One of the most frequently tested structures in TOEIC Part 5. Mastering the difference will directly improve your score.

⏱ ~15 min 🎯 Part 5 Focus 🔢 5 Quiz Questions

1. The Past Simple — A Closed Action in the Past

The Past Simple (Subject + V-ed / irregular) describes an action that is finished and located at a specific point in time. The time of the event is either stated or clearly implied from context.

When to use the Past Simple:

  • With a specific past time expression: yesterday, last week, in 2019, at 3 p.m., when I was young…
  • When telling a sequence of events in a story or report
  • When the action is completely finished and no connection to the present is implied
Formula — Past Simple
Subject + V-ed (regular) or irregular past form
✦ She signed the contract yesterday.
✦ We launched the product in March 2022.
✦ The CEO resigned last quarter.
TOEIC-Style Examples

✅ The company reported a 12% increase in revenue last fiscal year.

✅ Ms. Chen attended the conference in Tokyo in 2023.

❌ The company has reported a 12% increase last fiscal year. (specific past time → Past Simple required)

2. The Present Perfect — A Connection Between Past and Present

The Present Perfect (have/has + past participle) is used when the speaker wants to show a link between a past event and the present moment. The exact time is not the focus — what matters is the result, experience, or relevance now.

When to use the Present Perfect:

  • The action happened at an unspecified time: already, yet, just, ever, never, recently, so far, up to now…
  • An action that started in the past and continues to the present (with for / since)
  • Announcing news or recent changes
  • Describing life experiences without a specific time
Formula — Present Perfect
Subject + have/has + past participle
✦ She has signed the contract (recently, implication: it's done now).
✦ We have worked with this supplier for ten years.
✦ The CEO has just resigned. (recent news)
TOEIC-Style Examples

✅ The company has announced a new policy this week.

✅ We have been in business for over thirty years.

✅ Mr. Tanaka has never missed a deadline.

❌ She has signed the contract yesterday. (yesterday = specific time → Past Simple)

3. Key Signal Words — Your Fastest Clue on the TOEIC

Present Perfect SignalsPast Simple Signals
already, yet, just, stillyesterday, last week/month/year
ever, never, alwaysin + year (in 2019)
recently, lately, so farat + time (at 9 a.m.)
for + duration (for three years)ago (two months ago)
since + past point (since 2018)when + past clause
up to now, to date, thus farduring + period (during the meeting)

4. The Tricky Cases: for / since

Both for and since can accompany the Present Perfect, but they work differently:

  • for = duration (a length of time) → for five years, for a long time, for two months
  • since = starting point (a specific moment in the past) → since 2015, since January, since the merger
For vs Since

✅ She has managed the team for three years. (duration)

✅ She has managed the team since 2021. (starting point)

❌ She managed the team for three years. — This implies she no longer does. (Past Simple = finished)

5. Present Perfect Continuous — Duration Emphasised

When you want to stress the ongoing nature of an activity up to now, use have/has + been + V-ing:

Formula — Present Perfect Continuous
Subject + have/has been + V-ing
✦ We have been negotiating the contract for six weeks.
✦ The factory has been operating at full capacity since April.
💡

TOEIC Strategy: On Part 5, always scan the sentence for time-signal words first. If you see yesterday, last month, in + year, or ago → choose Past Simple. If you see already, yet, since, recently, for, never → choose Present Perfect. This eliminates wrong answers instantly.

⚠️

Common Mistake: Do not use Present Perfect with a specific finished time expression. "He has visited the office last Tuesday" is grammatically incorrect. The correct answer is "He visited the office last Tuesday."

Grammar · Lesson 2

Gerunds & Infinitives

Knowing which verb form to use after another verb is essential for TOEIC Part 5. This lesson covers the complete rules with business-context examples.

⏱ ~15 min 🎯 Part 5 Focus 🔢 5 Quiz Questions

1. What are Gerunds and Infinitives?

A Gerund is a verb used as a noun by adding -ing. It can function as a subject, object, or complement of a sentence.

An Infinitive is the base form of a verb preceded by to. It can act as a noun, adjective, or adverb.

Key Definitions
Gerund = Verb + -ing used as a noun ✦ Submitting the report on time is essential. (subject) ✦ The manager suggested postponing the meeting. (object) Infinitive = to + base verb ✦ We need to review the budget. (object) ✦ She agreed to sign the contract. (object)

2. Verbs Followed by Gerunds (-ing)

Certain verbs must be followed by a gerund. These are often verbs of stopping, avoiding, enjoying, or completing something already in process. Memorising this list is essential for TOEIC.

VerbExample (Business Context)
admitHe admitted making a mistake in the accounts.
avoidPlease avoid sending attachments over 10 MB.
considerThe board is considering expanding into new markets.
delay / postponeWe had to delay launching the product.
denyThe company denied violating the regulations.
discussWe discussed restructuring the department.
enjoyOur clients enjoy receiving personalised reports.
finish / completeShe finished drafting the proposal.
mindWould you mind rescheduling our appointment?
recommend / suggestI recommend reviewing the terms carefully.
riskDon't risk losing the client over a minor dispute.
stop / quitWe stopped using that supplier last year.
💡

Memory Trick: Gerund-only verbs usually describe something already happening or completed — you finish, stop, enjoy, or avoid things in progress.

3. Verbs Followed by Infinitives (to + base verb)

These verbs usually express intention, desire, decision, or expectation about a future action:

VerbExample (Business Context)
agreeBoth parties agreed to extend the deadline.
decideManagement decided to hire an external consultant.
expectWe expect to complete the project by Q3.
failThe contractor failed to deliver on time.
hope / plan / intendThe company hopes to increase market share by 15%.
manageShe managed to negotiate a better deal.
need / want / wishWe need to reduce operational costs.
offer / refuseThey offered to cover the shipping costs.
promiseThe vendor promised to replace the defective units.
seem / appearDemand appears to be declining in this sector.

4. Verbs That Take BOTH — With Meaning Changes

Two of the most tested verbs in TOEIC are remember and stop, which change meaning depending on whether they take a gerund or infinitive:

STOP

✅ We stopped working with that vendor. (= ended the activity)

✅ We stopped to work on the report. (= stopped [something else] in order to work)

REMEMBER

✅ She remembered sending the invoice. (= she has a memory of the past action)

✅ She remembered to send the invoice. (= she didn't forget to do it)

TRY

✅ Try calling the client directly. (= experiment with this approach)

✅ Try to reach the client before noon. (= make an effort)

5. Gerund as Subject — Common in TOEIC Part 5

When a verb form is used as the subject of a sentence, you must use the gerund, not the infinitive:

Gerund as Subject
✦ Networking regularly is key to career growth. ✦ Reviewing contracts carefully prevents disputes. ✦ Outsourcing IT support has saved the company money. ✦ Hiring internationally requires additional documentation.

6. Prepositions + Gerund

After any preposition (about, at, by, for, in, of, on, to, without…), always use the gerund:

Preposition + Gerund Examples

✅ She is interested in joining the project team.

✅ We are responsible for managing client relations.

✅ He succeeded in securing the contract.

✅ The deal was completed without consulting legal.

❌ She is interested in to join the team. (infinitive after preposition = WRONG)

⚠️

TOEIC Trap — "look forward to": The word "to" here is a preposition, NOT part of an infinitive. Always use the gerund: We look forward to hearing from you — NOT "to hear."

💡

TOEIC Strategy: When you see a blank after a verb, ask: Is the preceding verb one of the gerund-only verbs (avoid, suggest, consider, finish, mind…)? If yes → -ing. Does the sentence contain a preposition before the blank? If yes → -ing. Otherwise, check if the verb is an infinitive-only verb.

Grammar · Lesson 3

Prepositions of Time & Place

Prepositions are tested heavily in both Part 5 and Part 6. One wrong preposition can completely change the meaning of a business message.

⏱ ~15 min 🎯 Parts 5 & 6 Focus 🔢 5 Quiz Questions

1. The Big Three: AT / IN / ON (Time)

These three prepositions follow a logical hierarchy based on the size or precision of the time reference.

PrepositionTime UseExamples
ATExact times, specific pointsat 9:00 a.m., at noon, at midnight, at the end of, at the moment
ONDays and dateson Monday, on July 15th, on weekends, on New Year's Day
INLonger periods: months, years, seasons, parts of dayin January, in 2024, in the morning, in spring, in the third quarter
💡

The Zoom Rule: Think of zooming in on a timeline. "In" is far away (wide lens — year/month). "On" is mid-level (day/date). "At" is zoomed in all the way (exact moment). The more precise, the smaller the preposition.

2. Prepositions of Duration and Deadlines

These prepositions are frequently confused in business writing and are prime TOEIC targets:

PrepositionMeaningBusiness Example
FORA span / duration of timeThe factory has been closed for two weeks.
SINCEA starting point in the pastWe have been partners since 2010.
DURINGWithin a named periodDuring the conference, three deals were signed.
BYNo later than a deadlinePlease submit your report by Friday noon.
UNTIL / TILLContinuously up to a pointThe office is open until 8 p.m.
WITHINInside a time limitRespond within 24 hours of receiving this notice.
BY vs UNTIL — The Key Distinction

✅ By Friday = the deadline is Friday (action completed before or on Friday)

✅ Until Friday = continuously happening up to Friday (then stopping)

— "Submit the form by Friday." (one-time action with deadline)

— "The special rate is valid until Friday." (continuous state up to Friday)

❌ Please submit the form until Friday. (incorrect: implies continuous submitting)

3. The Big Three: AT / IN / ON (Place)

PrepositionPlace UseExamples
ATSpecific point or addressat the reception desk, at headquarters, at the airport, at 5 Park Avenue
ONSurface, floor, street nameon the third floor, on the table, on Main Street, on the left
INEnclosed space, city, countryin the conference room, in New York, in France, in the envelope
Place Examples in Business Context

✅ The annual meeting will be held at the Grand Hyatt Hotel.

✅ Your parcel is in the mailroom on the ground floor.

✅ Our new office is located on Fifth Avenue.

✅ The report is on your desk.

✅ I left the documents in the folder on the shelf.

4. Other High-Frequency Prepositions

PrepositionKey UseExample
BETWEENTwo people/things/timesThe meeting is between 2 and 4 p.m.
AMONGThree or moreThe bonus was divided among all employees.
DESPITE / IN SPITE OFContrast (+ noun/-ing)Despite the delay, the project was successful.
DUE TO / OWING TOReason (+ noun)Due to bad weather, the flight was cancelled.
ACCORDING TOSource of informationAccording to the report, sales rose 8%.
IN ADDITION TOAdding informationIn addition to the base salary, you receive benefits.
INSTEAD OFReplacing one thingWe will use email instead of fax.
IN CHARGE OFResponsibilityShe is in charge of vendor relations.

5. Fixed Prepositional Phrases You Must Know

These collocations are tested regularly. Memorise them as fixed chunks:

  • on behalf of — He signed the contract on behalf of the director.
  • in response to — In response to your inquiry, we are pleased to offer…
  • with regard to / regarding — With regard to your complaint, we have investigated…
  • at your earliest convenience — Please reply at your earliest convenience.
  • in the meantime — Your replacement will arrive Monday. In the meantime, use the spare unit.
  • on a regular / daily basis — Reports are submitted on a weekly basis.
  • at no additional charge / at no extra cost — The upgrade is available at no additional charge.
  • in accordance with — In accordance with company policy, all expenses must be pre-approved.
  • subject to — All prices are subject to change without notice.
  • in the event of / in case of — In the event of a cancellation, you will receive a full refund.
⚠️

Common TOEIC Traps:
— interested IN (not "about") something
— responsible FOR (not "of") an area
— apologise FOR (not "about") an inconvenience
— congratulate someone ON (not "for") their achievement
— comply WITH (not "to") regulations

💡

TOEIC Strategy: For Part 5 preposition questions, check whether the missing word comes after a specific adjective or verb with a fixed collocation (e.g., "responsible ___"). These are tested as vocabulary knowledge, not as grammar rules. Learn 30–40 key collocations and you will handle most preposition questions automatically.
